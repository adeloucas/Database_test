How DPI Helps Bring Collections Online
recovering the first two-thirds of human history.

A guide to DPI's methodology, from physical artifact to digital record.

Our Expectations

Modern Metrics

DPI aims to define a standard of practice for the publication of cuneiform objects—one that holds scholars responsible for the complete recording of these historical artifacts. Publishing a text is no longer enough; its digital object must be updated in tandem.

Our Practice

DPI's Gold Standard

DPI organizes around the principle that no cuneiform artifact is completely digitized until its textual record has been captured without loss of information. We call this the Gold Standard—a four-part framework covering institutional registration, digital documentation, physical recording, and complete textual reproduction.

Step 01

Bring Collections Online

DPI tracks collections that have catalogued and registered their cuneiform holdings, be it one artifact or hundreds of thousands.


In phase 2, we will offer services to aid institutions that lack the tools, expertise, or infrastructure to complete their catalogues independently.

Step 02

Alignment by the Gold Standard

DPI assesses each collection against a register of databases to measure the completeness of each artifact's digital record. We align physical artifacts with online data and identify where further work is needed to close these gaps.

Step 03

Evaluate Progress

DPI surfaces patterns at both macro and micro scales—what percentage of all cuneiform artifacts are accessible to the world, which institutions need the most support, and where bottlenecks are slowing progress.

Step 04

Lead Breakthrough Challenges

DPI produces machine learning challenges to address those bottlenecks—including sign-recognition technology and automatic translation. The goal is to move from 10% to 100% complete digitization of all cuneiform records.

Our Model

DPI's Workflow

Collections are assessed using FactGrid, made possible through Linked Open Data and international partnerships with experts and research projects, such as the CDLI, eBL, ORACC, BDTNS and ARCHIBAB.

Each artifact is evaluated on four levels, providing a metric that quantifies the completeness of each artifact's digital record and its institution's overall accessibility.

Test Case

The Yale Babylonian Collection

The Babylonian Collection at the Yale Peabody Museum holds 40,073 cuneiform artifacts—one of the largest such holdings in the world. Despite its records being among the most digitized, significant discrepancies remain across databases. Yale's own website associates 34,455 artifacts with CDLI and 38,284 with eBL; eBL's own count suggests 39,280 and CDLI suggests roughly 35,440. These figures must be reconciled before any record can be considered complete.

The transliteration picture is similarly inconsistent. CDLI reports 13,153 texts with transliteration; eBL reports 2,768—a maximum coverage of 38%. Translations are even sparser: CDLI lists 125, though this figure excludes the many translations held in databases that do not openly share data, such as ARCHIBAB (3,526 texts). This, too, must be addressed.
